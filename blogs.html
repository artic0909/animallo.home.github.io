Welcome to our Wildlife Encounter Services page, where your journey to connect with the natural world begins. At ANIMALLO, we are passionate about bringing people closer to the captivating wonders of the animal kingdom. Our expert guides and dedicated team ensure that you have an unforgettable and educational experience, fostering a deep appreciation for wildlife conservation.

Amazon Rainforest

The Amazon Rainforest in South America is one of the most biodiverse places on Earth. It is home to countless species of animals, including jaguars, sloths, capybaras, toucans, and a wide variety of amphibians, reptiles, and insects.

Sundarbans

The Sundarbans mangrove forest, located in the delta region of the Bay of Bengal, spans parts of Bangladesh and India. It is famous for being the habitat of the Bengal tiger, as well as numerous other species like crocodiles, spotted deer, and various bird species.

Borneo Rainforest

The Borneo rainforest, shared by Malaysia, Indonesia, and Brunei, is home to unique wildlife, including orangutans, pygmy elephants, proboscis monkeys, and a diverse array of bird species.

Congo Basin

The Congo Basin rainforest in Central Africa is known for its incredible biodiversity. It's inhabited by forest elephants, gorillas, chimpanzees, okapis, and various species of birds and reptiles.

Taiga

The taiga, or boreal forest, is the world's largest land biome, stretching across northern Europe, Asia, and North America. It's home to animals like wolves, moose, reindeer, lynx, and various bird species.

Daintree Rainforest

The Daintree Rainforest in Queensland, Australia, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. It's home to unique species like the southern cassowary, tree kangaroos, and many bird species.

Hoh Rainforest

Located in Olympic National Park, Washington, USA, the Hoh Rainforest is part of a temperate rainforest ecosystem and is inhabited by animals like Roosevelt elk, black bears, and various bird species.

Gir Forest National Park

This forest in Gujarat, India, is the last refuge of the Asiatic lion. It's also home to leopards, sambar deer, and various bird species.

Gombe Stream National Park

Located in Tanzania, this park is known for Jane Goodall's groundbreaking work with chimpanzees. It's home to a variety of primates and other wildlife.

Yosemite National Park

First protected in 1864, Yosemite National Park is best known for its waterfalls, but within its nearly 1,200 square miles, you can find deep valleys, grand meadows, ancient giant sequoias, a vast wilderness area, and much more.

Sumatra Rainforest

The 2.5 million hectare Tropical Rainforest Heritage of Sumatra site comprises three national parks: Gunung Leuser National Park, Kerinci Seblat National Park and Bukit Barisan Selatan National Park. The site holds the greatest potential for long-term conservation of the distinctive and diverse biota of Sumatra.

Madagascar Rainforest

The Madagascar lowland forests or Madagascar humid forests are a tropical moist broadleaf forest ecoregion found on the eastern coast of the island of Madagascar, home to a plant and animal mix that is 80 to 90% endemic.

African Elephant

African elephants are members of the genus Loxodonta comprising two living elephant species, the African bush elephant (L. africana) and the smaller African forest elephant (L. cyclotis). Both are social herbivores with grey skin.

African rhino

The black rhinoceros, black rhino or hook-lipped rhinoceros (Diceros bicornis) is a species of rhinoceros. Although the species is referred to as black, its colours vary from brown to grey. It is the only extant species of the genus Diceros.

Baby orangutan

Orangutans are great apes native to the rainforests of Indonesia and Malaysia. They are now found only in parts of Borneo and Sumatra, but during the Pleistocene they ranged throughout Southeast Asia and South China.

black footed cat

The black-footed cat (Felis nigripes), also called the small-spotted cat, is the smallest wild cat in Africa, having a head-and-body length of 35–52 cm (14–20 in). Despite its name, only the soles of its feet are black or dark brown.

cheetah

It is the fastest land animal, capable of running at 80 to 98 km/h (50 to 61 mph); as such, it has evolved specialized adaptations for speed, including a light build, long thin legs and a long tail.

gorilla

The largest of the great apes, gorillas are stocky animals with broad chests and shoulders, large, human-like hands, and small eyes set into hairless faces.

hedgehog

Although their eyesight is poor, they have good hearing and a well-developed sense of smell. Hedgehogs are good runners, proficient climbers and can even swim. If attacked they roll into a tight ball so only their spines are exposed.

black jaguar

Jaguars Have the Strongest Bite of the Cat Kingdom (Relative to Size) These majestic cats have a stocky, heavy build with robust canines and a massive head, allowing them a more powerful bite than any other large cat relative to its size.

koala

Koalas have poor vision and rely heavily on their other senses. They have excellent hearing which helps them detect predators and other koalas. They have an acute sense of smell which also helps them detect other koalas and their favourite food trees

leopard

Leopards are incredibly athletic and known for their climbing ability. They often carry food into trees to avoid losing it to scavengers like lions and hyenas. They are also fast and can run up to 36 mph. In comparison, the cheetah, the fastest land mammal, runs 50 to 80 mph.

lion

The lion has forever been a symbol of strength, power and ferocity. During WWF tours to Africa, seeing the majestic species up-close is sure to be a spine-tingling experience. 10. African lions are the most social of all big cats and live together in groups or “prides.”

mexican gray wolves

Roughly five feet in length, Mexican gray wolves generally weigh between 50 and 80 pounds. Their coats are buff, gray, and rust colored, often with distinguishing facial patterns. They have large heads with thick muzzles, bushy tails, oversized paws, and long legs.

moose

Moose are the largest of all the deer species in the world. Only male moose (bulls) have antlers. The particular shape differs from animal to animal and they are shed every year around November, being replaced with larger ones the forthcoming spring.

musk ox

Muskox (Ovibos moschatus) Uses. Muskoxen are valued for their meat, and about 260 animals a year are harvested by hunters. Muskoxen are also valued for wildlife viewing, for the artwork made from their horns, and for their soft underwool called qiviut, considered the world's warmest wool for its weight.

panda

Pandas subsist almost entirely on bamboo, eating from 26 to 84 pounds per day. Though solitary animals, pandas still communicate with one another through vocalization and scent marking. They spray urine, claw tree trunks and rub against objects to mark their paths.

deer

The tissues present in the antlers of a deer grow faster than any other tissue on the planet. Chinese water deer do not possess an antler. They are the only deer species that do not have antlers, they use a very long canine tooth that they show off to impress their female mates.

wombat

Wombats are short-legged, muscular quadrupedal marsupials of the family Vombatidae that are native to Australia. Living species are about 1 m (40 in) in length with small, stubby tails and weigh between 20 and 35 kg (44 and 77 lb).

zebra

Zebras are easily recognised by their bold black-and-white striping patterns. The coat appears to be white with black stripes, as indicated by the belly and legs when unstriped, but the skin is black.

dolphin

Dolphins vary in size depending on the species, ranging from about 4 to 30 feet in length. Dolphins give birth to live young. Gestation periods vary, but it's generally around 9-17 months. They are known for their strong mother-calf bonds. Dolphins are carnivores, primarily feeding on fish, squid, and crustaceans.

Shark

Sharks come in various sizes. The largest, like the whale shark, can reach lengths of over 40 feet, while others are smaller. Most sharks give birth to live young after internal fertilization. Some lay eggs, which hatch outside the mother's body. Sharks are apex predators, feeding on a range of prey including fish, seals, and other marine animals.

Clownfish

Clownfish are relatively small, usually around 2 to 4 inches in length. Clownfish exhibit protandry, where the largest individual becomes female. They lay eggs on a suitable surface and guard them until hatching. Clownfish are omnivores, consuming plankton and small invertebrates.

Sea Turtle

Sea turtles vary in size, with the largest species, the leatherback, reaching lengths of up to 10 feet. Female sea turtles return to beaches to lay their eggs. The gender of hatchlings is determined by temperature. Sea turtles are herbivores (green turtles) or omnivores (hawksbill turtles), eating seagrasses, algae, jellyfish.

Octopus

Octopus sizes vary widely; the giant Pacific octopus can reach lengths of over 20 feet, including its arms. Octopuses have short lifespans, reproducing only once. Males often die shortly after mating. Octopuses are carnivorous, preying on crustaceans, small fish, and other marine animals.

Penguin

Penguins come in various sizes, with the emperor penguin being the largest, reaching around 3 to 4 feet in height. Penguins lay eggs that they incubate on their feet. Both parents share incubation duties and care for the chicks. Penguins primarily eat fish, squid, and krill.

Seahorse

Seahorses are small, typically around 1 to 12 inches in length. Male seahorses carry and give birth to the young. Females deposit eggs in the male's brood pouch. Seahorses use their long snouts to suck up small crustaceans and plankton.

Jellyfish

Jellyfish sizes vary, with some being quite small and others having bell diameters exceeding 6 feet. Jellyfish have complex life cycles, involving both asexual and sexual reproduction stages.

Whale

Whales come in different sizes. The blue whale, the largest animal on Earth, can reach lengths of up to 100 feet. Whales give birth to live young after a gestation period that varies by species. Whales are filter feeders, consuming krill, plankton, and small fish.

Lobster

Lobsters vary in size, with some species reaching lengths of over 3 feet. Female lobsters carry eggs externally for several months until they hatch into larvae. Lobsters are scavengers, feeding on small fish, mollusks, and detritus.

Manatee

Manatees are large, typically ranging from 8 to 13 feet in length and weighing around 800 to 1,200 pounds. Manatees have a slow reproductive rate, with a gestation period of about a year. Manatees are herbivores, grazing on aquatic plants and seagrasses.

Starfish

Starfish sizes vary, with some species having arms that span up to 2 feet. Starfish can reproduce sexually by releasing eggs and sperm into the water, or asexually through regeneration. Starfish feed on a variety of prey, such as small fish, crustaceans.
